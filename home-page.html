Nouns

Definition

A noun is a word that names a person, place, thing, or idea. Nouns can be concrete (something physical that you can touch or see) or abstract (something that you can't see or touch, like an idea or emotion).

Example

  • Person: Ahmed, teacher, mother
  • Place: London, park, school
  • Idea: freedom, love, plan

Sentences

  • The book is on the table.
  • He is a student.
  • He loves Quran.

Types of Nouns

  • Common Nouns: Refer to general items, people or places (e.g. book, teacher, city)
  • Proper Nouns: specific names for people, places, or things and are always capitalized (e.g. Ahmed, Paris, Harry Potter)
  • Countable Nouns: things that can be counted (e.g. book, apple, student)
  • Uncountable Nouns: things that can't be counted (e.g. water, air, sugar)

Pronouns

Definition

Pronouns replace nouns to avoid repetition and make sentences clearer.

Sentences

  • I am a student.
  • You are my teacher.
  • He forgot his keys
  • She is a nurse.
  • It is a book.
  • We are students.
  • They are teachers.

Type of Pronouns

  • Personal Pronouns: I, you, he, she, it, we, they
  • Possessive Pronouns: mine, yours, his, hers, its, ours, theirs
  • Reflexive Pronouns: myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, themselves
  • Relative Pronouns: who, whom, whose, which, that
  • Demonstrative Pronouns: this, that, these, those
  • Indefinite Pronouns: someone, anyone, everybody, few, many

Adjectives

Definition

Adjectives describe or modify nouns and pronouns. They give more information about the noun or pronoun.

Example

  • Color: red, blue, green
  • Size: big, small, tall
  • Age: old, young, new
  • Shape: round, square, rectangular
  • Origin: American, French, Chinese
  • Material: wooden, plastic, metal
  • Qualities: beautiful, ugly, smart

Sentences

  • The park is empty.
  • He is a smart student.
  • They live in a big house.

Types of Adjectives

  • Descriptive Adjectives: Describe qualities or characteristics (e.g. beautiful, tall, old)
  • Quantitative Adjectives: Indicate the number or quantity (e.g. many, few, some)
  • Demonstrative Adjectives: Point out specific items (e.g. this, that, these)
  • Interrogative Adjectives: Used to ask questions (e.g. which, what, whose)
  • Possessive Adjectives: Show ownership or possession (e.g. my, your, his)

Verbs

Definition

A verb is a word that shows an action, something happening, or a state of being. Verbs are important because they tell us what the subject is doing or what is happening.

Example

  • Action Verb: run, eat, sleep
  • Linking Verb: be, seem, become, is, am, are, was, were
  • Helping Verb (Auxiliary Verbs): have, has, do, does, can, may, will (help form verb tenses and moods)

Sentences

  • He runs every morning.
  • He is a teacher.
  • They have a car.

Types of Verbs

  • Transitive Verbs: Require a direct object (e.g. She bought a car.)
  • Intransitive Verbs: Do not require a direct object (e.g. He sleeps well.)
  • Phrasal Verbs: Combinations of verbs and particles that create specific meanings (e.g. look up, give in).

Adverbs

Definition

Adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. They tell us how, when, where, why, or to what extent something happens.

Example

  • Manner: quickly, slowly, well
  • Place: here, there, everywhere
  • Time: now, later, yesterday
  • Frequency: always, never, often
  • Degree: very, too, quite

Sentences

  • He quickly ran to the store.
  • He is very handsome.
  • They never go to the park.

Types of Adverbs

  • Manner Adverbs: Tell us how something happens
  • Place Adverbs: Tell us where something happens
  • Time Adverbs: Tell us when something happens
  • Frequency Adverbs: Tell us how often something happens
  • Degree Adverbs: Tell us to what extent something happens

Prepositions

Definition

Prepositions are words that show relationships between other words in a sentence, usually involving time, place, direction, manner, or cause.

Types of Prepositions

  • Prepositions of Time
  • Prepositions of Place
  • Prepositions of Direction/Movement
  • Prepositions of Manner
  • Prepositions of Cause/Purpose
  • Prepositions of Agent or Instrument
  • Prepositions of Possession
  • Prepositions of Measure/Standard
  • Prepositions of Contrast/Concession
  • Prepositions of Condition

Prepositions of Time

These prepositions indicate when something happens.

  • The meeting starts at 9 AM
  • He was born on Monday.
  • We will visit you in December.
  • Finish your homework before dinner.
  • Let's meet after the movie.
  • He studied during the night.

Prepositions of Place

These prepositions show the location or position of something.

  • He is sitting in the room.
  • The book is on the table.
  • He is waiting at the bus stop.
  • The cat is hiding under the chair.
  • The keys are between the pillows.
  • The car is parked behind the building.
  • The restaurant is near the park.
  • The airplane flew above the clouds.
  • He sat beside his friend.

Prepositions of Direction/Movement

These prepositions describe the direction or movement towards something or away from it.

  • We walked to the store.
  • He came from the gym.
  • The cat jumped into the box.
  • He ran out of the house.
  • He moved towards the door.
  • They walked through the forest.
  • He swam across the river.
  • We walked along the beach.

Prepositions of Manner

These prepositions describe the manner or way in which something happens.

  • They traveled by car.
  • He wrote with a pen.
  • He acted like a fool.
  • He spoke in a loud voice.

Prepositions of Cause/Purpose

These prepositions indicate the reason or purpose for something.

  • He was praised for his bravery.
  • We stayed home because of the rain.
  • The game was canceled due to the storm.

Prepositions of Agent or Instrument

These prepositions show the person or object that causes or is used for an action.

  • The book was written by my father
  • He cut the paper with scissors.

Prepositions of Possession

These prepositions indicate ownership or relationship.

  • He is a friend of mine.
  • A house with a garden.
  • This pen belongs to her.

Prepositions of Measure/Standard

These prepositions are used to express quantities, measures, or standards.

  • We sell apples by the dozen.
  • He earns $20 at an hour.
  • The speed limit is 60 miles per hour.

Prepositions of Contrast/Concession

These prepositions express contrast or concession between two statements.

  • We managed to smile despite the pain.
  • He succeeded in spite of the obstacles.
  • They fought against all odds.

Prepositions of Condition

These prepositions are used to express the conditions under which something happens.

  • He is working under pressure.
  • You can't enter without a ticket.

Conjunctions

Definition

Conjunctions are words that connect other words, phrases, or clauses. They help to create complex sentences, by joining ideas and showing the relationship between them.

Types of Conjunctions

  • Coordinating Conjunctions
  • Subordinating Conjunctions
  • Correlative Conjunctions

Coordinating Conjunctions

These conjunctions connect words, phrases, or clauses of equal importance.

  • I need to study, for I have an exam tomorrow.
  • I like tea and coffee.
  • I want to go, but I can't.
  • Do you want tea or coffee?
  • I am tired, yet I can't sleep.
  • I am hungry, so I will eat.
  • I don't like tea, nor do I like coffee.

Subordinating Conjunctions

These conjunctions connect independent clauses to dependent clauses.

  • I will go if you go.
  • I will go when you go.
  • I will go before you go.
  • I will go after you go.
  • I will go because you go.
  • I will go although you go.
  • I will go while you go.

Correlative Conjunctions

These conjunctions are used in pairs to connect words, phrases, or clauses.

  • Either we go out or stay home.
  • Neither he nor she is coming.
  • Both he and she are coming.
  • Not only he but also she is coming.
  • Whether you go or stay, it's up to you.

Interjections

Definition

Interjections are words or phrases that express strong emotions or sudden bursts of feeling. They are used to convey excitement, surprise, anger, or other emotions. They are often followed by an exclamation mark (!) or a comma (,) when the feeling is less strong.

Example

  • Wow! (surprise)
  • Oh no! (disappointment)
  • Yay! (happiness)
  • Oops! (mistake)
  • Oh, (realization)
  • Hey, (getting attention)

Sentences

  • Wow! That's incredible!
  • Oh no! I forgot my keys.
  • Yay! We won the game!
  • Oops! I dropped my phone.
  • Oh, I forgot to bring my homework!
  • Hey, what's going on here?
